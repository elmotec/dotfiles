# The Scuttlebutt Method: Fisher's Research Methodology

Fisher's "scuttlebutt" method is his system for gathering ground-level intelligence about a company before investing. The name comes from the nautical term for the ship's water barrel — where sailors gathered and gossiped. Fisher found that talking to the right people reveals far more about a company than any financial report.

**Core insight**: Most of what you need to know about a company can be learned from people *outside* the company — customers, competitors, suppliers, ex-employees, scientists in related fields. They know the truth and are often willing to share it.

---

## THE SEQUENCE: NEVER VISIT MANAGEMENT FIRST

Fisher's non-negotiable rule: **Gather at least 50% of required knowledge BEFORE contacting management.**

Why this matters:
1. Without prior research, you don't know what questions to ask.
2. Management will not volunteer their own weaknesses. But if you already know about a weakness, a good management will confirm it and explain what they're doing about it.
3. Your estimate of management quality is distorted by which executives you happen to meet first. Scuttlebutt helps you know *which* executives matter most and whether to seek them out.
4. Companies give far more access and time to informed visitors. Walking in unprepared signals amateur status and gets you routed to a PR person, not decision-makers.

**Fisher's own ratio**: He bought into 1 out of every 40–50 companies he actually investigated — but he only investigated after extensive scuttlebutt had already made him reasonably confident. If you counted all companies he "considered," the ratio was closer to 1 in 250.

---

## STEP 1: FIND AN ORIGINAL LEAD

Sources Fisher used (ranked by reliability):
1. **Trusted investment professionals** with strong track records — a 1-hour conversation with a sharp investment analyst who has studied the company is enormously valuable.
2. **Industry executives and scientists** — people who understand the technical landscape of an industry can point to which companies have genuine competitive advantages.
3. **Selective use of brokerage reports** — only non-public, detailed reports from top-tier firms. Mass-market brokerage circulars are too superficial and often already known by the market.

**Initial filter (fast judgment)**:
- Is this company in a line of business with genuine long-term growth prospects?
- Is the competitive moat defensible — are there meaningful barriers to newcomers displacing this company?

If both answers are yes, proceed to investigation. If no, move on — great opportunities elsewhere.

---

## STEP 2: QUICK DOCUMENT REVIEW (BEFORE FIELDWORK)

Before making calls, do a brief document scan. Fisher specifically looked at:
- **SEC prospectus / annual report**: Product line breakdown by revenue, competitive dynamics, officer/major shareholder stock ownership (proxy statement), R&D expense, depreciation, profit margins, any abnormal costs in prior years.
- **Balance sheet**: General financial health and capitalization structure (not for deep analysis yet — just to understand the landscape).

**What Fisher did NOT do at this stage:**
- Spend hours on detailed year-by-year balance sheet analysis.
- Contact management.
- Ask every broker he knew for their opinion.

---

## STEP 3: AGGRESSIVE SCUTTLEBUTT FIELDWORK

Now make contact with everyone who has direct exposure to the company:

### Primary Sources

**Customers**
- Do they view the company as a preferred vendor?
- Is the product/service considered superior, equal, or inferior to competitors?
- How is the sales organization perceived? Helpful? Responsive? Technical?
- Would they increase or reduce business with this company?

**Competitors**
- What do they genuinely fear about this company?
- Where do they see themselves as stronger?
- How do they view this company's management and strategy?
- *Note*: Competitors are surprisingly candid about respected rivals.

**Suppliers**
- How does this company treat its vendors?
- Is it known for fair dealing or constantly squeezing for the lowest price?
- Is it a reliable, long-term partner or does it switch suppliers constantly?
- What do they know about the company's financial health and growth plans?

**Ex-employees**
- Why did they leave?
- How would they describe the culture and management style?
- What were the real weaknesses inside the company?
- What are the genuine strengths?
- *Note*: Ex-employees are often candid in ways current employees cannot be.

**Scientists and academics in related fields**
- How is this company's research viewed within the scientific community?
- Are its researchers respected?
- Is it working on genuinely important problems or incremental improvements?
- *Especially valuable for technology and pharmaceutical companies.*

**Industry consultants and bankers**
- Commercial bankers who serve the company can often provide introductions to hard-to-reach contacts.
- Consultants working in the industry have broad perspective on competitive positioning.

---

## STEP 4: USING BANKER INTRODUCTIONS

If you want to reach someone who won't talk to a cold caller:
1. Identify the commercial bank that serves the person you want to meet.
2. Contact the bank directly, explain you are doing investment research and want an introduction.
3. Be completely transparent about your purpose.
4. Most commercial bankers will help — they value relationships with serious investors.
5. People introduced by their own banker are far more likely to speak candidly.

**Fisher's rule**: Never quote anyone derogatorily by name. The moment sources fear being exposed, they stop talking.

---

## STEP 5: APPROACHING MANAGEMENT (ONLY AFTER STEP 3)

Once scuttlebutt has given you a substantial picture, visit management with your knowledge in hand.

**How to get a good introduction to management:**
- Major customers with purchasing relationships.
- Large institutional shareholders.
- The company's investment banker.
- A mutual friend in the industry respected by the management.

**Why introductions matter**: Management gives far more time and candor to visitors who come with credibility. Cold approaches get routed to investor relations staff, not actual decision-makers.

**What good management does during a visit:**
- Answers questions about weak points as fully as about strong points.
- Admits when there are problems and explains the plan to fix them.
- Doesn't over-promise.

**What this visit is for:**
- Confirming or disconfirming your scuttlebutt picture.
- Getting answers to specific questions you couldn't answer externally.
- Assessing management integrity and transparency first-hand.
- Identifying which of your earlier fears were real vs. overblown.

**What to look for in management's responses:**
- If you mention a weakness you discovered in scuttlebutt, does management acknowledge it honestly and explain what they're doing? (Good sign.)
- Or do they deny it exists? (Red flag — either they don't know their own company, or they're not being straight with you.)

---

## KEY SCUTTLEBUTT PRINCIPLES

**Quantity over any single source**: No single source is fully reliable. Build your picture from 5–10+ independent contacts. Patterns that appear across multiple sources are trustworthy.

**Look for what's NOT said**: If multiple people avoid discussing a topic, that silence is informative.

**Competitor praise is the highest signal**: When competitors say something positive about a rival, that's extremely credible. They have every incentive to be negative.

**Focus your questions on the 15 Points**: Structure your conversations around what you need to know — don't have aimless conversations.

**Discard if not enough information is available**: Fisher was willing to walk away from a potential investment if scuttlebutt couldn't be assembled. "To make big money on investments it is unnecessary to get some answer to every investment that might be considered. What is necessary is to get the right answer a large proportion of the very small number of times actual purchases are made."

## WHAT SCUTTLEBUTT REVEALS BEST

| Point | How well scuttlebutt reveals it |
|-------|---------------------------------|
| Sales organization quality | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — customers and competitors know exactly |
| R&D effectiveness | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — scientific community and ex-researchers are candid |
| Labor relations | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — widely known in local business community |
| Management integrity | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — industry reputation spreads quickly |
| Long-range vs short-range profit outlook | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ — suppliers and customers see this directly |
| Executive relations depth | ⭐⭐⭐ — harder, but multiple executive conversations reveal it |
| Cost accounting controls | ⭐⭐ — hard to assess externally, requires management visit |
| Market potential (Point 1) | ⭐⭐⭐ — industry experts can assess, but requires judgment |

## MODERN SCUTTLEBUTT SOURCES

Fisher wrote in the 1950s. Today, equivalent sources include:
- **Glassdoor/Blind reviews**: Employee sentiment (modern equivalent of ex-employee conversations)
- **LinkedIn**: Career trajectories of employees — are top people staying or leaving?
- **Industry conferences and trade publications**: Technical credibility of the company
- **Customer reviews and forums**: Quality and reliability of products/services
- **Supplier relationships**: Procurement forums, industry associations
- **Earnings call transcripts + analyst Q&A**: How does management respond to hard questions?
- **SEC filings for related parties**: Proxy statements reveal insider ownership, related-party transactions (Point 15)
- **Patent databases**: Volume and quality of R&D output
- **Job postings**: What skills is the company hiring for? (Reveals strategic direction)

The tools have changed. The method hasn't.
