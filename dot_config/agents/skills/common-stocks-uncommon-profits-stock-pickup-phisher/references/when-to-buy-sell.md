# When to Buy and When to Sell: Fisher's Timing Rules

---

## WHEN TO BUY

### The Core Principle: Ignore Economic Forecasting

Fisher's most counterintuitive rule: **Do not base buying decisions on predictions about the economy or stock market.**

His reasoning:
- In the 1950s (and still today), economic forecasting is more art than science — "about as far along as chemistry was during the days of alchemy."
- Opposing forecasts from equally credentialed experts abound. Read back issues of any financial publication — you'll find confident predictions that were spectacularly wrong.
- The mental effort the investment world puts into economic forecasting could be far better deployed.
- *Exception*: When the economy is so clearly overheated (like 1929) that even rough forecasting is reliable. But such moments occur perhaps once per decade.

**Fisher's substitute for market timing**: Instead of asking "is the economy favorable?", ask: "Is this specific, outstanding company experiencing a *temporary* problem that has depressed its stock price?"

---

### The Four Types of Buying Opportunities

#### Type 1: New Plant Shake-Down Problems

**The pattern:**
1. A great company announces a revolutionary new manufacturing plant.
2. Investors bid up the stock on excitement.
3. The plant takes far longer to reach operating efficiency than expected ("bugs in the machinery").
4. Unexpected costs depress quarterly earnings.
5. Wall Street declares management has "blundered."
6. The stock falls to multi-year lows.
7. The plant finally operates smoothly — but the *next* quarter still shows low profits from heavy sales investment to build volume.
8. **At this point — the stock is potentially a sensational buy.**

**Why it's an opportunity**: Once a new process works at commercial scale, the second, third, and fourth plants can be built with minimal shake-down costs. Each replication dramatically expands earnings. The first-plant pain is a one-time cost; the benefits compound for years.

**Key test**: Are the problems *temporary* (engineering challenges being solved) or *permanent* (the product doesn't work / the market isn't there)?

**Example**: American Cyanamid's Fortier, Louisiana plant (1954) — massive over-run costs, stock fell sharply. Those who understood the temporary nature of the problem bought at 45.5. Within 5 years the stock had roughly tripled.

#### Type 2: Temporary Corporate Troubles

**The pattern:**
1. A fundamentally excellent company faces a combination of unusual, temporary problems (labor dispute, management personnel change, product misjudgment).
2. Earnings fall sharply.
3. Stock falls significantly.
4. Able management recognizes the problems and has a clear plan to fix them.
5. While the plan is being implemented (which takes months, not days), more bad news surfaces.
6. **Buying Point A**: Initial drop, plan announced but not yet showing results.
7. More short-term trouble (e.g., a second strike during recovery).
8. **Buying Point B**: Stock falls again on new bad news despite recovery being close.

**Key test**: Are the problems being actively addressed by capable management? Is the timeline months, not years?

**How to apply**: "The investor should be thoroughly sure in his own mind that these troubles are temporary rather than permanent. Then if these troubles have produced a significant decline in the price of the affected stock and give promise of being solved in a matter of months rather than years, he will probably be on pretty safe ground in considering that this is a time when the stock may be bought."

#### Type 3: Capital Expansion Leverage

In capital-intensive industries (chemicals, manufacturing):
- Company has $10M plant running profitably.
- Engineers identify that for an additional $1.5M (15% more capital), output can increase 40%.
- The incremental profit margin on that extra 40% output is dramatically higher (almost no additional overhead).
- **Buying just before this improvement reaches earnings** can provide an excellent entry.

#### Type 4: War Scares and Market Panics

Fisher's counterintuitive rule: **Buy more during war scares, not less.**

His evidence:
- Every major conflict in the 20th century caused stocks to fall sharply at the outset.
- At the conclusion of *every* conflict, stocks were trading vastly higher than before the war began.
- Modern war forces governments to spend far beyond tax revenues → monetary inflation → stock prices (expressed in units of money) rise.
- "War is always bearish on money." Selling stocks to get into cash during a war scare is "extreme financial lunacy."

**Fisher's rule**: If you've been considering buying a stock and a war scare hits, buy. If actual war breaks out, buy more aggressively (but buy companies whose products/services remain in demand, or which can convert to wartime production).

## WHEN NOT TO BUY (COMMON TIMING MISTAKES)

### Mistake 1: Waiting for Economic Clarity

"I'll buy when the economy looks better." The economy rarely looks perfectly clear. Meanwhile, the best companies keep growing. You miss years of compounding.

### Mistake 2: Thinking a High P/E Means Overpriced

A company that has consistently traded at 2× the market P/E for 30 years and continues to deliver is NOT overpriced relative to its own history. The error is comparing its P/E to average stocks rather than asking: "Given its future growth rate, what P/E will it command in 5 years?"

If a great company typically commands 25–30× earnings, and earnings are expected to double in 5 years, the stock may be no more "expensive" today than 5 years ago — it's just discounting the same premium for ongoing excellence.

### Mistake 3: The "Hasn't Gone Up Yet" Trap

Many investors choose stock B (which "hasn't gone up yet") over stock A (which has already risen significantly). This is backwards. Past price rise has no predictive value. What matters is future earnings power. A 600% gainer that continues growing is a better buy than a flat stock with mediocre prospects.

### Mistake 4: Overweighting Historical Price Ranges

"The stock used to sell at $35. It's at $60 now — too expensive." Past price ranges are largely meaningless. What matters is whether the business is more or less valuable *today* relative to other opportunities. A company that has added brilliant management, new product lines, and superior research may be worth 4× more today than three years ago. Historical prices don't tell you this.

### Mistake 5: Fixating on Historical EPS

Past EPS has almost no significance for future investment returns. What matters is the *next* 5 years of earnings. Companies in transition (improving management, new products ramping) may have lackluster past EPS but explosive future potential — exactly when Fisher wants to buy.

## WHEN TO SELL

Fisher's most important selling rule: **If the job was correctly done when buying, the time to sell is almost never.**

But "almost never" has three specific exceptions:

### Valid Reason 1: You Made a Mistake at Purchase

If you bought a stock that — on honest reflection — never fully qualified under the 15 Points, sell it. Even at a loss. Even at a small profit.

Why: "More money has probably been lost by investors holding a stock they really did not want until they could 'at least come out even' than from any other single reason."

The psychology: Ego makes us not want to admit mistakes. But the difference between a 20% loss and a 5% gain on a bad holding is trivial compared to the compounding gain you miss by not redeploying into a good holding. Sell quickly; redeploy quickly.

**After any loss**: Review it carefully to understand exactly what was misjudged. Each loss is a lesson that prevents the same mistake in the future.

### Valid Reason 2: The Company Has Deteriorated

If a company once fully qualified on the 15 Points but no longer does — sell immediately.

**The two main ways companies deteriorate:**
1. **Management deterioration**: Success breeds complacency; or new executives don't maintain the standards of their predecessors. "Smugness, complacency, or inertia replace the former drive and ingenuity."
2. **Market exhaustion**: The company has fully captured its market and will now only grow at the rate of the general economy. No new products in the pipeline.

**Test for market exhaustion**: Will per-share earnings at the *next* business cycle peak be meaningfully higher than the current peak? If yes, hold. If no, it's time to sell.

**On management deterioration**: "When any of these things happen the affected stock should be sold at once, regardless of how good the general market may look or how big the capital gains tax may be."

**On tax considerations**: Fisher is emphatic — capital gains taxes should almost *never* be the reason to hold a deteriorating company. The tax cost of switching is minor compared to years of compounding in a better company.

### Valid Reason 3: You Found a Clearly Better Opportunity

If you find another company that is demonstrably more attractive than one you hold — with confidence in your analysis — it may be worth switching even after paying capital gains tax.

**Key caution**: Be very sure. You know your current holding well; you're less familiar with the new one. Unknown risks in the new situation can eliminate the apparent advantage.

**Fisher's standard**: The new opportunity should be clearly superior — not just marginally better. A company growing at 20% per year vs. one at 12% per year, over a decade, justifies the switch. But the case must be clear-cut.

## WHAT IS NOT A VALID REASON TO SELL

### "The Market Looks Expensive / A Correction is Coming"

If you couldn't reliably predict the top, you won't reliably call the bottom to buy back in. Fisher's evidence: He has seen investors dispose of outstanding holdings repeatedly due to bear market fears. In most cases: (a) the bear market never came, or (b) they failed to buy back before the stock rose above their selling price.

"I have not seen one time in ten when the investor actually got back into the same shares before they had gone up above his selling price."

### "The Stock Has Gone Up A Lot"

Outstanding companies don't have a built-in ceiling. A company that has delivered 500% gains and continues to grow its earnings through new products is a *better* investment than when you bought it, not a worse one. Selling just because the stock has risen is selling the company's future for a guaranteed exit.

Fisher's analogy: It's like selling your contract with a college classmate who is rapidly becoming a highly paid executive, just because someone offers you 600% of your original investment. Why would you give up the ongoing right to a quarter of his earnings just because you've already made a big gain?

### "The Stock is Overpriced Based on Current Earnings"

See "Mistake 2" in the buying section. Outstanding companies command premium P/Es indefinitely. 35× earnings for a company that will double earnings in 5 years may not be overpriced at all. Momentarily getting 35% ahead of fair value is irrelevant when the company will compound for another decade.

## SUMMARY: THE SELLING DISCIPLINE

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Bought in error — never fully qualified | Sell immediately, take the loss |
| Management has deteriorated | Sell immediately |
| Growth market exhausted, no next wave | Sell (can be gradual) |
| Found clearly superior opportunity | Consider selling (be very certain) |
| General market looks expensive | Do NOT sell |
| Stock has risen a lot | Do NOT sell (if business is still excellent) |
| Short-term earnings dip from temporary trouble | Do NOT sell |
| War scare or market panic | Do NOT sell (consider buying more) |
| Large capital gains tax would result | Irrelevant to the sell decision |
