# Fisher's Don'ts: Ten Common Investor Mistakes

Fisher dedicated two chapters to mistakes that cost investors dearly. These are not obscure errors — they are systematic patterns he observed repeatedly among otherwise intelligent people.

---

## THE FIVE DON'TS (CHAPTER 8)

### Don't 1: Don't Buy Into Promotional Companies

**The mistake**: Investing in companies that are still in the concept/startup phase, before they have established operations.

**Why it's dangerous**:
- In an established company, you can observe production, sales, accounting, management teamwork. You can get opinions from qualified observers.
- In a promotional company, you're reading a blueprint and guessing. This allows a far greater probability of error.
- Promotional companies are often dominated by one or two brilliant people who are superb at one thing (inventing, selling) but lack other essential skills.

**Fisher's rule**: "No matter how appealing promotional companies may seem at first glance, I believe their financing should always be left to specialized groups." Individual investors — those who cannot supply management talent to fill gaps as problems emerge — should not invest in promotional-stage companies.

**There are enough outstanding opportunities among established companies that this rule is worth following strictly.**

---

### Don't 2: Don't Ignore Good Stocks Just Because They Trade Over-the-Counter

**Context**: In Fisher's era (1950s–60s), many investors thought only NYSE-listed stocks were safe.

**Fisher's finding**: The better over-the-counter stocks often have comparable or superior liquidity to stocks on smaller regional exchanges. The quality of the business matters far more than where it trades.

**Modern application**: Don't avoid small-cap or ADR-listed companies simply because they're not on a major index. Evaluate the business, not the listing venue.

---

### Don't 3: Don't Buy a Stock Because You Like the Annual Report's "Tone"

**The mistake**: Being swayed by well-written, attractively formatted, optimistic annual reports.

**The reality**: Annual reports are marketing documents designed to build goodwill. They are written to put the company's best foot forward. The public relations department often writes the CEO's letter. Nice photographs do not indicate a cohesive management team.

**Fisher's rule**: Go beyond the annual report to the underlying facts — especially to scuttlebutt. The tone of the report tells you about the PR department, not the company.

**One exception**: If a company's annual report fails to give proper information on matters of real significance to investors, that lack of transparency *is* a signal — a negative one (see Point 14 about management clamming up).

---

### Don't 4: Don't Assume a High P/E Means Growth Has Been "Discounted"

**The mistake**: Seeing a stock at 30× earnings (vs. market average of 15×) and assuming "all the good news is already priced in."

**Why this is wrong**:
- If a company has *consistently* traded at 2× the market P/E for 30 years because of its sustained excellence, and it continues to be excellent, it will likely trade at 2× market P/E five years from now too.
- So if earnings double in 5 years, the stock price should also double — even at the same "elevated" relative P/E.
- The error is using the *wrong benchmark* (market average P/E) instead of the company's *own historical P/E range*.

**Fisher's test**: The key question is whether the growth is a one-time spurt (in which case the elevated P/E *will* compress back to average when growth ends) or sustained ongoing growth (in which case the elevated P/E is permanent and justified).

**Implication**: Some of the stocks that "appear highest priced may, upon analysis, be the biggest bargains."

---

### Don't 5: Don't Quibble Over Eighths and Quarters

**The mistake**: Refusing to pay the current market price, instead placing a limit order a fraction of a point lower.

**Fisher's actual example**: An investor wanted 100 shares at 35¼. He refused to pay it and set a limit at 35. The stock never hit 35 again. 25 years later (adjusting for splits) the stock was at 500+. He saved $50 and missed ~$46,500.

**The math**: $46,500 is 930× $50. You would have to save $50 from 930 different "eighths" just to break even on this one decision.

**Fisher's rule**: For small investors wanting a few hundred shares — if the stock is the right one and the price is reasonable, buy at the market. The difference of an eighth or quarter is utterly insignificant against a multi-year holding.

**For large investors** wanting thousands of shares: Work with your broker on a strategy to accumulate shares without moving the market — but still give the broker latitude on price rather than setting rigid limits.

## FIVE MORE DON'TS (CHAPTER 9)

### Don't 6: Don't Overdiversify

**The common error**: Owning 25–50 stocks to "be safe."

**Why over-diversification hurts**:
- Forces you to allocate capital to companies you don't know well.
- Impossible to keep meaningful track of 30+ companies.
- Dilutes your best ideas with mediocre ones.
- "A little bit of a great many can never be more than a poor substitute for a few of the outstanding."

**Fisher's paradox**: Investors are so afraid of "too few eggs in one basket" that they put too little into companies they thoroughly understand and too much into companies they know nothing about. Owning an unknown company is *more* dangerous than owning a concentrated position in a well-understood excellent company.

**Fisher's minimum diversification guidelines:**

| Company Type | Maximum % of Original Capital Per Position | Minimum # of Positions |
|---|---|---|
| Type A: Large, established growth stocks (like Dow, Du Pont, IBM) | 20% per position | 5 stocks minimum |
| Type B: Mid-sized growth companies ($15–100M revenue, strong management team) | 10% per position | 10 stocks minimum |
| Type C: Small, high-risk/high-reward companies | 5% per position | 20 stocks minimum (to get adequate diversification of this risk) |

**Note**: These are *minimums* — it's fine to hold fewer stocks if they're truly outstanding. The constraint is knowledge, not safety theater.

**Fisher's practical observation**: In practice, most serious investors' real problem is finding enough great investments, not having too many. A long list of securities is usually a sign of an investor who is unsure of themselves, not a brilliant diversifier.

**Key exception**: If one holding grows dramatically to become 40% of your portfolio, don't automatically sell it for diversification. The growth happened because the business is excellent. Rebalancing away from your winners is often a mistake.

### Don't 7: Don't Sell Because of War Scares

Already covered in detail in `references/when-to-buy-sell.md`.

**Summary**: Every 20th-century war ended with stocks far higher than they started. War is bearish on money (inflation), not on stocks. Selling during war scares is "extreme financial lunacy." Buy during war scares; buy more when actual hostilities begin.

---

### Don't 8: Don't Forget That Facts Change — Historical Statistics Are Often Misleading

**Fisher's "Gilbert and Sullivan" warning**: Like "flowers that bloom in the spring, tra-la," historical price ranges and historical EPS "have nothing to do with the case."

**The mistake — historical price ranges**: Looking at where a stock traded for the past 5 years to determine a "fair" price today. Stock prices reflect the consensus value of the business *at the time they were set*. The business may be fundamentally more or less valuable today.

**The mistake — historical EPS**: Using past 5 years' earnings as the primary valuation metric. This is like driving using only the rearview mirror. "What is necessary is to find the right answer a large proportion of the very small number of times actual purchases are made." 

The next 5 years of earnings are what matter, not the last 5.

**Why analysts feed you backward-looking statistics**: Because historical facts are indisputably correct — no one can be embarrassed by reporting what already happened. Forward-looking analysis can be proven wrong, so there's an incentive to stick to safe, historical data. Don't confuse factual precision with investment relevance.

### Don't 9: Don't Confuse Insider Selling with a "Sell" Signal

Fisher discusses a specific case (Texas Instruments, 1956):
- Officers sold shares.
- Wall Street: "We agree with them — sell!"
- Reality: The officers were selling small portions of heavily concentrated positions for estate planning, diversification, and liquidity reasons entirely unrelated to their view of the company's future.
- The company proceeded to rise 1000%+ in the following years.

**Fisher's nuanced view on insider selling**:
- Small sales by officers of genuinely outstanding companies are often prudent personal financial management, not bearish signals.
- Officers of great growth companies frequently become multi-millionaires through their holdings. It is entirely rational to take a little off the table for estate planning, diversification, or cash needs.
- The *amount* and *pattern* matter: one officer selling 2% of their position is very different from five insiders selling large blocks simultaneously.

**What *does* matter**:
- If *all* key insiders are selling *large* portions simultaneously.
- If insiders are buying in bad times (a positive signal).

### Don't 10: Don't Fail to Consider Opportunity Cost

This is implicit throughout Fisher's writing but deserves explicit statement:

**The mistake**: Holding a mediocre investment because it might "come back" or because selling would mean admitting a mistake.

**Fisher's calculation**: If you have $100K in a stock you bought in error, and you sell at a 20% loss ($80K) and redeploy into an outstanding growth stock that triples over 5 years, you end at $240K. If you hold the mistake and it merely recovers to $100K over 5 years, you end at $100K. The "shame" of the 20% loss cost you $140K in opportunity.

**The habit to build**: Regularly ask whether each holding, if it were cash today, would be your first choice to buy. If not, consider why you're holding it.
