# The Fifteen Points: Fisher's Growth Stock Criteria

This is Fisher's complete framework for evaluating whether a company qualifies as an outstanding long-term investment. A company that scores well on most of these points — especially integrity — can produce gains of several hundred percent over a decade.

---

## POINT 1: Does the company have products/services with sufficient market potential to sustain sizable sales growth for at least several years?

**What to look for:**
- Is the market large AND still early-stage? A big market that's nearly saturated (like TV sets in 1960) is a one-time opportunity, not a long-term growth story.
- Does the company have realistic room to grow for the next 3–5 years AND beyond?
- Avoid companies with one product surge that will plateau (e.g., a radio manufacturer when TV adoption peaked).

**Fisher's two archetypes of great growth:**
- "Fortunate and Able" companies: In great industries that grew bigger than anyone imagined (Alcoa / aluminum). They had luck AND skill.
- "Fortunate Because They Are Able" companies: Created their fortune through superior management skill — they steered into growth, didn't just ride it (Du Pont: from blasting powder to nylon, cellophane, neoprene).

**Red flags:**
- Industry growth is a one-time surge (pandemic-era demand, a single regulation change)
- Market is becoming saturated
- No clear path to the *next* wave of growth after current products mature

**Key question**: Is this company's growth likely to continue for *several* multi-year periods, or is it a one-time event?

---

## POINT 2: Does management have the determination to develop new products/processes to sustain growth *after* current products are exploited?

**What to look for:**
- Does management actively recognize they will "grow up" to their current market and need new ones?
- Is there an active R&D pipeline aimed at adjacent markets?
- Contrast: a company enjoying current success with no plan for next-generation products vs. one like Motorola, which used its electronics expertise to build two-way communications, semiconductors, and stereophonic audio *before* the TV market plateaued.

**Key distinction from Point 1**: Point 1 is a *fact* (how big is the current opportunity). Point 2 is *management attitude* (are they building for what comes next?).

**Best pattern**: R&D that expands from the company's existing expertise — "clusters of trees each growing from their own trunk." Scattered R&D in unrelated industries is less reliable.

---

## POINT 3: How effective is the company's R&D relative to its size?

**What to look for:**
- R&D spending as % of sales is a *starting clue only* — don't over-rely on it. Companies define R&D expense very differently.
- What matters: actual output — how many profitable new products has this R&D produced in the last decade?
- Team coordination matters as much as talent: research must link tightly to production and sales. Brilliant researchers creating unbuildable or unsellable products are wasteful.
- Beware "crash programs" — managements that constantly restart research projects waste money and demoralize researchers.
- Government-funded defense R&D has uneven value: best when the technical knowledge can be transferred to commercial applications (Texas Instruments, Ampex did this brilliantly).

**The best proxy**: Has this R&D engine produced a meaningful stream of profitable new products in the past 10 years? If yes, it will likely continue.

---

## POINT 4: Does the company have an above-average sales organization?

**What to look for:**
- The best products won't reach their potential without skilled distribution. This is one of the three pillars of corporate success (alongside production and research).
- Look at how much the company invests in training its salespeople. IBM in the 1960s had salespeople spend a *third* of their time in company-run schools.
- Dow Chemical selected and trained salespeople with the same rigor as research chemists.
- Companies that rely only on product quality without strong distribution are vulnerable.

**Best source of information**: Scuttlebutt. Competitors and customers know exactly how good a company's sales organization is and are usually willing to say. This is one of the easiest points to investigate externally.

**Red flag**: A company with great products but a weak or mediocre sales force.

---

## POINT 5: Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin?

**What to look for:**
- Study profit margins over multiple years, not just one year. Boom years inflate margins for all companies, especially weaker ones.
- The *strongest* companies have the best margins across economic cycles, not just during peaks.
- Marginal companies (low profit margins) show dramatic margin expansion in good years — but equally dramatic collapse in bad years. They are not the right long-term investment.

**Important exception**: A company deliberately sacrificing near-term margins to fund growth (through heavy R&D or aggressive market expansion) may still qualify — but you must be absolutely certain the low margin is *intentional and temporary*, not structural weakness.

**Red flag**: Persistently thin margins that don't reflect a deliberate growth strategy. "Marginal companies" rarely produce the best long-term returns.

---

## POINT 6: What is the company doing to maintain or improve profit margins?

**What to look for:**
- In today's economy, cost pressures (wages, materials, taxes) are constant. How is the company fighting back?
- The *worst* approach: relying solely on price increases. When an industry raises prices, it attracts competition and eventually oversupply erodes the advantage (see: aluminum 1956, copper cycle).
- The *best* approach: active internal cost reduction — capital improvement programs, automation, process redesign, logistics optimization.
- Does the company have a dedicated engineering/operations department specifically focused on cost reduction?

**What to ask management**: What specific programs are in place to reduce costs? What has been saved in the past 3 years through these programs?

---

## POINT 7: Does the company have outstanding labor and personnel relations?

**Indicators of good labor relations:**
- Non-union status in a unionized area (e.g., Motorola in Chicago, Texas Instruments in Dallas) — workers choose not to unionize when they feel fairly treated.
- If unionized, a history of reasonable, mutually respectful relations.
- Low labor turnover relative to the industry.
- Long waiting lists of applicants wanting to work there.
- Grievances settled quickly.
- Above-average wages for the area (a company earning high profits while paying below-market wages is storing up trouble).

**Indicators of poor relations:**
- Frequent and prolonged strikes.
- High turnover.
- Management that treats workers as interchangeable — no sense of responsibility.
- Workers hired or dismissed in large masses based on slight changes in business outlook.

**Note**: Absence of strikes alone does NOT mean good relations. Some companies have no strikes because workers fear the consequences, not because they're satisfied.

---

## POINT 8: Does the company have outstanding executive relations?

**What to look for:**
- Executives at all levels have confidence in the president/chairman.
- Promotions based on ability, not family connections or favoritism.
- Salary reviews happen regularly; executives don't have to demand raises.
- Salaries in line with industry standards.
- Management fills positions from within first; only hires outside when truly no internal candidate exists.
- Factionalism and internal friction are actively managed and minimized.
- Top brass do NOT personally interfere with day-to-day operating matters.

**How to assess**: Talk with executives at multiple levels of the organization. The further a company departs from these standards, the less likely it is to be an outstanding investment.

---

## POINT 9: Does the company have depth of management?

**What to look for:**
- A small company can do well under one brilliant leader for a while — but all humans are finite.
- Once annual sales reach $15–40 million (in 1950s dollars), one-man management becomes a growth constraint.
- Key test: Does each level of management have genuine authority to carry out their function? Or do senior leaders constantly override and micromanage?
- Does top management welcome suggestions from junior personnel, even when those suggestions criticize current practices?

**Red flag**: Organizations where the "top brass personally interfere with and try to handle routine day-to-day operating matters." This prevents developing the next generation of leaders.

---

## POINT 10: How good are the company's cost analysis and accounting controls?

**What to look for:**
- Can management break down costs with sufficient accuracy to know the true cost of each product and each operational step?
- Without this, the company cannot: set pricing intelligently, know which products deserve promotion, identify operations running at a loss.

**Limitation**: This is hard for an outside investor to evaluate precisely. The best proxy is general management quality — a company excellent in most other respects is likely adequate here too.

**Red flag**: Obvious operational inefficiency, or situations where the company seems unaware of its own cost structure.

---

## POINT 11: Are there industry-specific factors that give important clues about the company's competitive position?

**Examples of industry-specific factors to consider:**
- **Retail**: Quality of real estate/lease management — lease costs as % of sales.
- **Insurance-intensive industries**: Overall insurance cost relative to peers reveals management skill in handling people, inventory, and property.
- **Technology companies**: Patent position — important for young companies as protection while building customer relationships and manufacturing scale; less critical for large established companies (patents expire, and depending on them is a weakness, not a strength).
- **Capital-intensive industries**: Credit management efficiency.

**Fisher's view on patents**: Large companies shouldn't depend primarily on patents for competitive moats — patents expire, and there are usually multiple ways to achieve the same result. Constant *engineering leadership* is more durable than static patent protection.

---

## POINT 12: Does the company have a long-range or short-range outlook on profits?

**Long-range indicators:**
- Management willing to forgo some current profit to maintain supplier/customer goodwill.
- Pays vendors above contract price when they've had unexpected costs — ensures reliable supply when needed.
- Goes to extra trouble to help customers in a bind even at short-term expense.
- Builds relationships that produce larger long-run profits over years.

**Short-range indicators (red flags):**
- Constantly makes sharpest possible deals with every supplier.
- Never gives anything extra to customers.
- Focused on maximizing this quarter's numbers.

**Best source**: Scuttlebutt from suppliers and customers. This pattern shows up clearly in how a company is talked about in its industry.

---

## POINT 13: Will required equity financing cancel out shareholders' benefit from anticipated growth?

**What to evaluate:**
- Does the company have sufficient cash + borrowing capacity to fund the next several years of growth?
- If equity financing will be needed soon: will the resulting share dilution significantly reduce per-share earnings growth for current shareholders?
- For convertible securities and warrants: calculate dilution as if *fully converted* when assessing real share count.

**Fisher's principle**: Financial strength matters, but never buy a stock *solely* because of a strong balance sheet if the company fails on the other 14 points. Conversely, don't avoid a company excellent in other respects just because it needs some capital — if it's truly exceptional, future financing will occur at much higher prices, minimizing dilution.

**Red flag**: Management planning equity financing that will be dilutive *within the next few years* at prices not much above current levels. This reflects poor financial planning.

---

## POINT 14: Does management talk freely when things go badly, or do they "clam up"?

**What to look for:**
- Great companies, run by great management, still have failures, plant shake-downs, disappointing products. This is inevitable.
- The test is how management communicates during difficulties.
- Outstanding management is as forthcoming in bad times as in good — they explain what went wrong, what they're doing about it, and give realistic timelines.

**Why "clamming up" is a red flag:**
- May signal the problem isn't being addressed.
- May reflect a management that feels no accountability to shareholders.
- May reflect panic rather than problem-solving.

**Fisher's rule**: Exclude from investment any company that withholds or tries to hide bad news.

---

## POINT 15: Does the company have management of unquestionable integrity?

**This is Fisher's most important and non-negotiable criterion.**

**Red flags for management integrity:**
- Key officers on payroll at salaries far above market value for their work.
- Company assets rented or sold to insiders at above-market rates.
- Vendor business routed through brokerages owned by insiders.
- Excessive, self-serving stock option grants that far exceed fair reward for services.

**Fisher's rule**: "Regardless of how high the rating may be in all other matters, however, if there is a serious question of the lack of a strong management sense of trusteeship for stockholders, the investor should never seriously consider participating in such an enterprise."

**This is the only point where a single ❌ disqualifies the investment completely**, regardless of scores on the other 14.

**Note**: A company can fall down on almost any other single point and still be an interesting investment if it scores highly on all the rest. But integrity failure is absolute.

---

## APPLYING THE 15 POINTS: PRACTICAL NOTES

**What sources to use for each point:**

| Points | Best Source |
|--------|------------|
| 1, 2, 3 | Management + industry experts + scuttlebutt from R&D community |
| 4 | Customers + competitors (very accessible via scuttlebutt) |
| 5, 6, 10 | Financial filings + direct management discussion |
| 7, 8, 9 | Scuttlebutt from employees + executives at multiple levels |
| 11 | Industry-specific contacts |
| 12 | Suppliers + customers |
| 13 | Financial filings (calculate diluted shares) |
| 14 | Track record of press releases, earnings calls in bad periods |
| 15 | Proxy statements + scuttlebutt from insiders and ex-employees |

**Important**: Points 1–4 (business growth potential) and 15 (integrity) are the most critical. Start your evaluation there.
